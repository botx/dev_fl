1. Regulation

Enhance Regulatory Guidelines to Provide Greater Flexibility and Accommodate Emerging Risks

Review and update the supervisory framework and regulations​

Regulatory Impact Assessment (RIA) and Risk-Proportionality concepts will be applied to evaluate the impacts of regulations on all stakeholders based on their risk level. By adopting a principle-based approach, regulatory guidelines will be flexible and adaptable to changing environments and accommodate changes in business models and players. The amended regulation will ensure the payment system's stability, security, and resilience to risks and foster innovation development without hindering business providers. The improvement of the supervisory framework and guidelines under the RIA principles can be divided into 3 phases as follows:

Short-term​

Clarify and reestablish an understanding of regulatory intents and compliance, such as guidelines on the notification of service additions or alterations, to ensure appropriate practices consistent with the principles or intention of the regulations through direct discussion with business providers and preparing questions and answers attached to the notification in the third quarter of 2022.

Medium-term​

Amend related notifications to reduce regulatory burdens on business providers, such as permitting e-money service providers to conduct predefined businesses without seeking permission on a case-by-case basis. Efforts also include enhancing business management and operations to be on par with international standards, transparent, and fair, for instance, revising requirements on the qualifications and prohibited characteristics of business providers, directors, persons with managerial power, and major shareholders of the business providers; and improving market conduct regulations for designated payment business providers. The revision will be carried out in 2022.

Long-term​

Consider amending the Payment Systems Act and related notifications and guidelines, such as those related to licensing, maintenance of money received in advance, oversight of different business providers, and KYC requirements, to be commensurate with the level of transaction risk. The improvements are intended to provide more flexible supervision of payment systems and services in response to the changing contexts, business models, and business environment. These initiatives will take place between 2023 and 2024.

Issue regulatory guidelines keeping pace with new technologies and risks in the digital age​

To timely manage risks and threats associated with online transactions while encouraging innovation development, regulations governing the following issues will be improved.

New technologies and risks​

The BOT promotes a regulatory framework consistent with international standards, particularly IT/cyber regulations while ensuring regulatory compliance with other relevant laws, such as online fraud prevention, money laundering, and personal data protection, to boost confidence, security, and continuity of operations. As for technology, the regulatory guidelines under the IT/cyber risk governance framework have been revised to align more closely with the service provider's risks and to prevent such risks from being passed on to other sectors in the financial systems. Further, a self-regulated supervisory approach will be applied to monitor associated risks in a timely manner. In addition, fraud risk monitoring and management will be enhanced for more rigorous user protection, better fraud monitoring, and faster responses to reduce the likelihood of fraud victimization and minimize potential losses. Moreover, the BOT will promote cooperation and information exchange among the financial sector and related agencies, such as payment service providers, the Anti-Money Laundering Office (AMLO), the Royal Thai Police, and the Electronic Transactions Development Agency (ETDA). The subsequent collaboration will focus on building a fraud database for surveillance and supporting the formulation of BOT regulations.

The provision of digital services to enhance cash management efficiency​

Although the BOT encourages digital payments as a preferred option and discourages cash usage, cash remains a necessity. The infrastructures and related services, therefore, require improvement to increase efficiency and reduce overall system costs during the transition. The BOT will review regulations on cash deposits and withdrawals via banking agents, such as counters and kiosks widely distributed in various communities, as well as modern devices that provide withdrawals and deposits services (e.g., EDC machines, mobile phones, smart kiosks, and white-label smart machines, which offer superior service capabilities to ATMs). The revised regulation will help reduce the costs associated with redundant ATMs and promote financial access in remote areas. Further, the BOT will collaborate with the Ministry of Finance and specialized financial institutions to enhance efficiency and access to cash withdrawal and deposit services for people in remote areas, as outlined in the revised regulations.

Supervision of digital assets in payments​

The BOT will consider the supervisory approach to the usage of digital assets related to payments by focusing on promoting innovations that benefit the business and public sectors, enhancing payment system efficiency while ensuring appropriate risk oversight and consumer protection, and preventing the impact on the stability of the financial and payment systems.
