Executive Summary

Significance and Development of Payment Systems​

Payment systems are a crucial mechanism ensuring smooth and efficient economic and financial transactions and supporting the country's economic development. Over the past two decades, Thai payment systems have laid the foundations for the continuous transition to e-payments in terms of infrastructure, laws, and regulations. Digital payment infrastructure has evolved dramatically in the past five years. Significant developments are the PromptPay system and the Thai QR Code standard. These infrastructures not only support payments for the public, business, and government sectors but also support the extension of innovations and a wide range of domestic and cross-border payment services, resulting in extensive use of digital payment services. The public digital payments have grown fivefold, from 63 times per person per year in 2017 to 312 times per person per year in 2021. Covid-19 is another major accelerator of digital payments, leading Thailand to become one of the region's payment leaders and one of the global leaders initiating the development of Wholesale Central Bank Digital Currency (CBDC) to prepare for the financial world of the future.

Challenges and Stakeholders’ Opinions​

There are numerous digital payment channels and services available today. Thailand is transitioning from a cash-intensive society to a less-cash society while maintaining the right balance of access to financial and payment services. Business providers have offered a wide range of digital payment services to fulfill users' needs in terms of online and in-store payments, card and non-card payments, and public, business, and government sectors. These services are constantly evolving. The BOT encourages diversity to provide alternatives to different service users based on sound risk management and adequate users' digital financial literacy.

Nonetheless, technology and digital trends will continue to evolve. Thailand's payment systems must continue to advance to keep up with such progress. Various challenges must be considered when developing payment system directions, such as the emergence of new technologies, the complexity and diversity of services and payment business providers, the rise of cyber threats and various fraudulent scams, and the growth of "digital assets," a new financial model. Therefore, formulating the payment directions requires a complete assessment of multiple factors. The BOT has invited comments and suggestions from interested parties, including the general public. A summary of valuable recommendations for the development directions is as follows:

  1. There should be a payment system governance structure to ensure the balanced participation of various bank and non-bank players in the effective implementation of the BOT policies under a systematic and standard agreement. Additional roles would include driving widespread adoption, resolving system issues or disputes, and overseeing the management of domestic critical payment infrastructures for maximum mutual benefits, at attractive and fair prices, subject to adequate and appropriate risk supervision.

  2. Raising the target for Thai digital payments usage to 2.5 times in three years is feasible as Thais increasingly turn to digital payments. Efforts should be made to create proactive driving forces for digital payment adoption and to encourage all sectors to work together to accelerate the continued reduction of cash and cheque usage. At the same time, attention must be paid to those not ready for digital payments in parallel with education on how to use the service safely.

  3. Emphasis should be placed on personal data protection and data governance, along with data utilization. Due to the considerable number of users, the BOT should lead efforts to integrate payment data, such as connecting with government applications.

  4. Apply retail central bank digital currency (Retail CBDC) to financial services, such as conditional government welfare payments, to ensure efficient and reliable payments. Offline payments without a smartphone and internet connection should be possible. Wallet topping up should be simple, with multiple channels and no need for a bank account, with multiple levels of e-KYC and multiple forms of ID being critical success factors.

Directions for Development of Payment Systems Under the New Financial Sector Landscape of Thailand in a 3-years period (2022–2024)​

Based on past developments and achievements, challenges, and stakeholders' feedback, the BOT has formulated the Payment Directional paper on "Directions for Development of Payment Systems Under the New Financial Sector Landscape of Thailand" over a three-year period (2022–2024) to guide the payment development directions under the principles of Openness, Inclusivity, and Resiliency. The BOT envisions Digital Payments as the preferred option for all user groups, improving quality of life, enhancing competitiveness, and transitioning Thailand to a "less-cash" society. This strategic direction focuses on strengthening the Thai payment systems to facilitate the financial sector's efficient and sustainable transition into the digital economy and to foster the development of digital payment services by various service providers to provide users with alternatives. Key strategic plans are as follows:

1. Openness: Leverage shared payment infrastructure and information to increase efficiency and promote competition​

Payment systems enable access to payment infrastructure, payment data, and utilization of technologies in innovation development to create an environment conducive to competition in 3 areas:

  1. Open Infrastructure aims to leverage interoperable payment infrastructure to reach a diverse range of players and public and business users under appropriate governance and pricing structures and prepare for a new stage of the financial sector. Key initiatives include (1) developing a financial and digital payments infrastructure for businesses, or the PromptBiz system; (2) enhancing the usage of international and central standards in payment systems; (3) studying, developing, and testing the Central Bank Digital Currency (CBDC) infrastructure for domestic and cross-border payments such as wholesale CBDC (mBridge); and (4) establishing a payment systems governance structure and reviewing the payment systems and services pricing structure.

  2. Open Data aims to promote the utilization of digital payment data under adequate data governance in terms of personal data protection and data security. Actions include integrating payment data in the financial and government sectors and developing new payment data structures as part of the Regulatory Data Transformation (RDT) project.

  3. Open Competition focuses on creating an environment conducive to competition among payment service providers and fostering the adoption of new technologies to develop domestic and cross-border payment services and innovations. Plans include improving guidelines and standards to allow various service providers to share critical payment infrastructure and promoting Thailand’s role in cross-border payments regionally and internationally.

2. Inclusivity: Promote accessibility and digital financial literacy​

The BOT envisions digital payment to be the most preferred choice for Thais, thereby pushing for more comprehensive digital payment services and channels and promoting confidence in digital payments through the following initiatives.

  1. Adoption focuses on promoting digital payment usage in the public, business, and government sectors by expanding services in public sector applications, closing the gaps in business payments, and soliciting government support.

  2. Literacy focuses on building confidence in digital payments by promoting understanding and raising awareness of safe usage. The implementation plans include adjusting the knowledge-building approach to be more proactive to achieve broader and tangible results and fostering cooperation at the financial sector level and between the financial and the government sectors.

3. Resiliency: Adopt flexible regulatory supervision adaptable to changes and risks in the digital age​

In a digital world where technology, service models, and consumer behavior are rapidly changing, the oversight of payment service providers needs to be sufficiently flexible to allow service providers to adapt as well as to strike the right balance between fostering technological innovation developments and managing and responding to risks in the digital age.

  1. Regulation emphasizes the flexibility of payment systems regulations to accommodate innovations and new risks. Initiatives include reviewing the payment systems regulatory framework by applying the Regulatory Impact Assessment (RIA) and risk-proportionality concepts and developing regulations to facilitate new financial models.

  2. Supervision aims to improve the supervisory framework to accommodate the digital world by employing supervisory technology, reshaping governance models and methods, creating cooperation mechanisms with relevant agencies, and strengthening supervisory personnel's capabilities to keep up with the changes.

Benefits From Payment Systems Development Under the New Financial Landscape of Thailand​

  1. Thai payment systems The payment infrastructures are stable, efficient, in line with the economic development, accessible to various players, and foster payment service innovations that meet users’ needs and provide valuable data. The payment governance structure is appropriate and serves as an important engine of development. Payment supervision is flexible, effective, and capable of addressing new risks in the digital age.

  2. The General Public Various digital payment services are available. The public can choose the service that suits their needs based on sufficient knowledge and understanding, take simple precautions against digital threats, and are given options and opportunities to learn and adapt.

  3. Businesses Payment services are convenient, address trade business and digital payment needs, and help enhance business efficiency by saving time, reducing paperwork, and lowering costs. Digital payments also improve access to funding, boosting business potential and, ultimately, supporting the country's competitiveness.

  4. Payment Service Providers Payment service providers are presented with more business opportunities, enhanced competitiveness, and abilities to adapt to the digital financial world through flexible regulations in favor of innovation development without impeding service delivery and under the timely supervision of emerging risks in the digital era.
