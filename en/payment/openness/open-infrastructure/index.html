1. Open Infrastructure

Open Infrastructure: Develop shared and interoperable payment infrastructures under a good governance structure and a reasonable pricing structure

Origin of the development: Payment services are not responsive to the business sector's needs. Unfair access to infrastructures exists. Governance mechanisms are deficient and ineffective.

  • Payment infrastructure is essential for developing services that meet the needs of users across all sectors. Thai retail payments have previously been highly successful owing to the critical infrastructure, namely the PromptPay system. Digital payments for the business sector, however, still require the proper infrastructure to satisfy the need for end-to-end integration of trade transactions and payments.
  • Bank and non-bank payment service providers continue to face cost and arrangement disparities when accessing existing infrastructures. This stifles competition and hinders overall payment system efficiency improvement.
  • Currently, no clear governance structure is in place in the payment systems to support the efficient management of infrastructure services and the transmission of the BOT's payment policies, as well as to ensure the involvement of relevant parties, to establish clear agreements and standards, and to manage the infrastructure landscape for maximum utilization.
Principles
  • What we wish to see Payment infrastructures are up-to-standards, stable, secure, interoperable, accessible to various service providers at an affordable price, and driven by an effective payment system governance structure. Such infrastructures foster competition in the development of innovation and financial services to better serve the public and business needs, facilitating and accelerating Thailand's transition into the digital economy and a less-cash society.
  • What we do not wish to see Payment infrastructures are fragmented or redundant to the point of inefficiency or are so concentrated that they pose a concentration risk. The pricing structures do not reflect actual costs, while shared usage is limited. As a result, such infrastructures will discourage competition in innovation and payment services development. In addition, there is no governance mechanism to support the effective and appropriate implementation of the BOT's policies.

Payment infrastructure development is divided into two parts:

  • Hard infrastructure refers to the development of infrastructure for the business sector, in addition to well-developed retail payment systems such as the PromptPay and Thai QR Code.
  • Soft infrastructure focuses on developing and implementing central standards to promote interoperability, lower costs, and increase efficiencies of connection and business operations of various service providers, leading to the development of seamless services. Soft infrastructure development also includes enhancing payment systems governance by arranging for collaboration, coordination, and development expansion, which will reinforce the strengths of the Thai payment systems.

Open Infrastructure Key Initiatives 2022–2024​

1.1 Develop digital financial and payment infrastructure for businesses (the PromptBiz system)​

Concept and Principles of the "PromptBiz System"​

The PromptBiz system is a unified infrastructure for trade and payment data in digital business processes leveraging the ISO 20022 international message standard. The system eliminates the current problem of excessive paperwork, which leads to high costs, time-consuming follow-up and document review, and possible errors. Such infrastructure can help increase efficiency and reduce costs.

Guiding Principles

  • Improve accessibility to promote competition among service providers and to support various service extensions (open infrastructure).
  • Comply with standards and support interoperability (Standard & interoperability)
  • Meet both large and small business needs and accommodate future growth (scalability & innovation)
  • Establish a governance structure and promote widespread adoption (governance & incentive structure)

International Success Various countries have successfully developed e-invoice platforms, such as the United Kingdom, France, and the United States.

  • In the UK, the cost of document handling per transaction was reduced by more than 65%.
  • In the US, payments received on time increased more than double.
  • In France, the courier costs were reduced by 96%, and the total processing time decreased from 15 days to 3 days.

PromptBiz System Development Plan​

The plan is divided into two parts: trade and payment services and digital supply chain finance.

  • Part 1: Trade and Payment Services Trade transactions such as invoice presentment and e-Tax invoice issuance will be linked to payments, which will contain trade information and connect to the e-Receipt service. The first phase is the development of core services, namely (1) e-Invoice delivery service that connects to the Tax Service Providers (TSP) and (2) e-Receipt/e-Tax Invoice data exchange service with payment functionality via the Bulk Payment system. The system is expected to be completed in April 2023. The next phase will extend the linkage to the D-Business platform1 to expand services to individual entrepreneurs and to support real-time or near real-time and high-value transactions. Further, a study will be conducted to explore the feasibility of developing a Digital Cheque (D-Cheque) as an alternative to meet the needs of businesses requiring cheque usage.

  • Part 2: Digital Supply Chain Finance Efforts will focus on developing a mechanism to support business lending, particularly for SMEs, by utilizing trade and payment data from Part 1. The first phase is the development of invoice verification and double financing examination services, which began operation on December 15, 2021. In the early stage, the system still requires data submission. Once Part 1 is completed, data will be transmitted through the PromptBiz system, after which other service extensions will be developed.

    In the initial phase, large corporates and SMEs (juristic persons only) who currently use Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) systems or software that can digitally process trade documents and payments can apply for the PromptBiz services through participating pilot banks. The next phase will expand the PromptBiz services scope, service providers, and users to facilitate individual entrepreneurs. Large corporates and SMEs applying for the PromptBiz services through participating banks can use their corporate ERP systems by upgrading their document or data delivery systems according to the PromptBiz connection standards, based on ETDA Recommendation on ICT Standard for Electronic Transactions: Trade Services Message Standard (3-2017).

Remark: In the future, the BOT will consider exploring the connection between the Domestic Business Platform in the PromptBiz system and the National Digital Trade Platform (NDTP).

1.2 Elevate the adoption of international and central standards in payment systems​

Concept and Principles​

The BOT encourages the adoption of international and central standards in payment systems. Emphasis is placed on leveraging the ISO 20022 message standards to enable payment data to contain other relevant information and employing the API technology to support connections with other critical financial infrastructures and create opportunities for future financial innovations.

Key Initiatives

  1. The BAHTNET - ISO 20022 (High-Value Payment System) Project seeks to improve the BAHTNET system by introducing the ISO 20022 international payment message standard to support automated processes and more efficient transaction verification. In addition, the system will be able to handle additional payment-related information, for instance, the sender and receiver details (such as English name, Thai name, personal/juristic person identification number, address, account number, and account type), the purpose of funds transfer, business data and tax data.) The improvement will enable more accurate transaction reconciliation and verification, promote interoperability with other critical infrastructures, and create opportunities for innovation. The service is expected to commence in 2022.

  2. The BAHTNET API HUB Project entails the development of the API2 Gateway, an infrastructure enabling connection to the BAHTNET system and supporting various digital financial services with straight-through-processing (STP) capabilities. By using the API technology with the ISO 20022 message standards, the infrastructure supports interoperability with other important domestic infrastructures and the extension of financial innovations, for example, funds transfer services, securities settlement services, high-value payments via PromptBiz, and near real-time third-party funds transfer services. The BAHTNET API HUB will be the primary channel for domestic large-value funds transfer and is anticipated to be available by the end of 2023.

  1. The ISO 20022 Migration (Retail Payment Systems) project involves perfecting the existing payment infrastructures, namely, the Bulk Payment system and the PromptPay system, to better accommodate the public and business needs. Both are anticipated to be completed by 2023. In addition, the QR Payment system (merchant-presented mode) is upgraded to increase efficiency in the paying/receiving funds with a planned launch date in 2024. The next phase will aim to improve the Cross-bank Bill Payment system for the business sector and enhance the Bill Alert system to build on the PromptPay services for businesses and increase the efficiency of the PromptPay's funds transfer services for individuals.

1.3 Establish a payment system governance structure and a reasonable pricing structure​

Concept and Principles​

Previously, Thai digital payments have grown exponentially with diverse linkages. The payment governance structure, however, has not been updated in time to reflect the changing context. Consequently, policy transmission is hampered due to insufficient stakeholder involvement. Rules and regulations become impediments to promoting a level playing field. Meanwhile, the landscape of payment infrastructure services reveals redundancy and concentration, leading to underutilization. Therefore, establishing an appropriate governance structure will improve the efficiency of infrastructure management and the BOT's payment policy transmission as the payment services have mutually agreed upon rules and standards, leading to better services for the public and business sectors.

Key Initiatives

  1. Establish the Payment Strategy Forum (PSF) The PSF will allow all relevant sectors to participate in the effective implementation of the BOT policies introduced by the Payment Systems Committee (PSC). The group will also provide recommendations on the direction of payment development and the development of payment services and innovations that will benefit end-users. The PSF will deliberate on the following issues:

    • Directions for payment systems and service developments and effective policy implementation
    • Essential standard guidelines for domestic and cross-border services
    • The adoption of international standards in the Thai payment systems
    • Communication and user education strategies to promote digital payments

    The composition of the PFS will be drawn from interested parties, including representatives from payment business providers, users, and governments who have knowledge and experience in the payment systems in the fields of payment business, technology, and law. In addition, experts or representatives from specific agencies may be present to support the relevant agendas. The PSF will have a joint secretariat of representatives from the BOT and payment system service providers.

  2. Establish a Central Payment Services Office (CPSO) The CPSO will serve as a working group for managing major payment services following international practices. The office will be responsible for setting guidelines and standards, including business, legal, technical, and operational rules, gathering information, and improving payment system services. The CPSO will also function as a coordinator and representative in domestic and international collaboration. Initially, the main services under the CPSO’s purview will be those widely adopted and providing linkages domestically and internationally, namely, the PromptPay, the Thai QR Payment, and the PromptBiz services.

    CPSO Roles and Responsibilities

    • Issue business rules, terms of service, pricing structure, and technical standards
    • Set access criteria and requirements for service applications
    • Push forward continuous improvements of related products and services
    • Review and track compliance with the above rules and requirements

  3. Review the management of payment system infrastructures and the payment services pricing structure to enhance efficiency and elevate important payment infrastructures to the domestic infrastructures. Key initiatives include promoting efficient utilization of infrastructures, removing redundancy or concentration that might pose systemic risks, providing open access to more service providers according to their risk levels at an affordable and fair price, and fostering payment innovations that harness technology and data.

Guiding Principles: Payment Infrastructure
  • Accommodate diverse service providers and interoperability
  • Enhance the efficiency of overall payment systems and foster innovation development and the digital economy in Thailand
  • Provide good governance and sound risk management according to international standards
  • Encourage competent human capital
  • Establish reasonable pricing structure supportive of long-term utilization

Regarding the pricing structure review, the BOT continues to advocate for pricing payment infrastructure services based on market mechanisms while keeping in mind the best interests of users and the nation. The BOT also encourages users in the public and business sectors to reduce their cash and cheque usage and opt for digital payments as a preferred payment option. The guiding principles are as follows.

Guiding Principles: Payment Infrastructure Pricing Structure
  • Reflect costs, risks, and service models and promote competition according to market mechanisms
  • Incentivize digital payment services as a preferred option and reduce cash and cheque usage
  • Retail users, as well as low-income, remote, and disadvantaged individuals, have choices in accessing financial services
  • Create an enabling environment for the connectivity and the development of shared infrastructures

Under the Payments Strategic Direction 2022-2024, the pricing and fee structures for cash and cheque transactions and important payment infrastructures will be reviewed according to the above principles.

Thais' Cash and Cheque Usage Behavior necessitated a change to reduce the country's costs

Thais use cash for 87 percent of their daily spending3. The annual cost of using cash is approximately 47 billion baht4. As for cheque usage, despite a steady decline in volume over the past five years, 77 percent of cheque usage was for payments of less than 100,000 baht5, for which the availability of digital payment services as alternatives can help reduce such costs.

Going forward: The BOT will discuss the establishment of the PFS and the CPSO with relevant parties to finalize the composition, roles, and responsibilities by studying international models and practices in Thailand to support the implementation.

Promote Digital ID, Digital Signature, and Digital Contract in Financial Services

The goal is to expand financial services and digital payment extensively. In Thailand, the efforts have progressed sequentially, beginning with the introduction of Digital ID by the National Digital ID Co., Ltd. (NDID) to provide identity authentication and verification services, followed by the issuance of standard guidelines for digital signature- and digital contract- by the Electronic Transactions Development Agency (ETDA). The next phase will focus on expanding the services to other products to provide better service experiences. The BOT will drive these development efforts in collaboration with the ETDA and other service providers.

  1. Promote and expand digital ID usage by extending the scope to accommodate business users and encouraging the use of a corporate digital ID. The service will help reduce the processes and complexity of financial transactions, such as the authorization to open a juristic person account, which the system development is scheduled to begin in 2022. Further, efforts to encourage widespread adoption of digital IDs will continue by ensuring that standards related to public and private digital ID services are consistent, allowing users in all sectors to verify their identities when transacting with different agencies.

  2. Expand the use of digital signature and digital contract in financial transactions. The efforts will not only add convenience and choices in financial transactions or contracts but also help reduce processing costs (e.g., document preparation time and travel expenses) while maintaining the security and legitimacy of the processes. For example, the application of digital technology in financial assistance and debt restructuring allows business providers to opt for a digital integration method by enabling debtors to accept agreements with a PIN or an e-signature. The service is set to begin pilot testing in 2022 and extend to the digital document creation and signing of other financial contract documents.

Increases Payment System Capabilities to Support the Digital Economy

The financial infrastructure supporting the digital world needs to be connected, multi-functional, and capable of handling large volumes of transactions to support convenient, fast, seamless, cost-effective economic and financial transactions at affordable prices and provide safe and reliable services.

The payment systems that play a critical role in facilitating the country's financial transactions should be capable of handling funds transfer, payments, clearing, and settlement. Further, the systems should have other capabilities that help enhance the efficiency of the payment systems, for example, facilitating real-time transactions, data exchanges, liquidity management, and providing 24/7 services. In addition, the systems should be able to connect to other domestic infrastructures to accommodate connected payment services for individuals, businesses, financial institutions, and government agencies.

Therefore, taking a holistic view of payment infrastructure and other key financial infrastructures and collaborating in the development of such infrastructures are essential to ensuring a sustainable entry into the digital economy.

Prepare for a new financial world: Exploring the application of CBDC (Central Bank Digital Currency)

Technology has altered the financial world in many dimensions. Most notably is the Distributed Ledger Technology (DLT), where decentralized data storage allows network members to exchange data, coordinate, and transact directly with one another, in addition to permitting the application of Smart contract. The adoption of DLT in the financial sector will enhance the efficiency of remittances and the functioning of the financial and payment infrastructures. For this reason, many central banks have studied the application of DLT in central banking. The BOT also attaches great importance to the study and development of the BOT-issued digital currency (CBDC) and has been one of the leading central banks to initiate such development studies since 2017. In 2021, PwC ranked the BOT as the world’s most mature wholesale CBDC effort.

However, at this moment, the BOT has no plans to issue a CBDC for broader use. As with many leading central banks, the development of the CBDC is still in the study and testing stage to ensure that both benefits and risks are understood. The advancement of the CBDC does not, in any way, block or delay the introduction of technology to the development of new payment models.

  1. Wholesale CBDC for the Financial Sector In collaboration with financial institutions, the BOT initiated "Project Inthanon" in 2017 to explore the feasibility and efficiency of CBDC in the financial institution sector. The project has progressed successively and has recently been extended for cross-border funds transfers under the Inthanon-LionRock project, a joint project with the Hong Kong Monetary Authority. The partnership has further expanded to the Central Bank of the United Arab Emirates and the Digital Currency Institute of the People's Bank of China, resulting in a new project known as the Multiple Central Bank Digital Currency Bridge Project (mBridge). The mBridge project aims to develop a prototype for multi-currency cross-border funds transfers while emphasizing enhancing the efficiency of liquidity management, maintaining the privacy of financial transactions, and testing the capability of the DLT system to handle large transaction volumes.

  2. Retail CBDC for the Public and Business sectors Retail CBDC is a development effort to explore a CBDC model that facilitates innovation development through programming conditions on the CBDC (programmability) with security, credibility, and suitability for usage in the public and business sectors. The BOT will conduct limited testing from late 2022 to mid-2023. The results will be used to expand the development into the private sector in the next stage.
